£25k to £80k — How Take-Home Pay Compares

2026/27 Tax Year — England, Wales & Northern Ireland

Take-home pay doesn't scale in a straight line with salary. Once you cross £50,270, every extra pound is taxed at 40% instead of 20%, so the gap between your gross salary and what actually lands in your account widens fast. The five salaries below span that gap — from £25k, well within the basic rate band, up to £80k, firmly into higher-rate tax — using confirmed 2026/27 Income Tax and National Insurance rates: a Personal Allowance of £12,570, basic rate at 20% up to £50,270, higher rate at 40% above that, and Class 1 employee NI at 8% (2% above £50,270). All figures are rounded to the nearest pound.

Notice how the effective tax rate climbs faster once you're above £50,270 — between £45k and £60k it jumps from 20.2% to 24.4%, a bigger increase than between £25k and £45k combined. That's the higher-rate band at work. These are estimates based on England, Wales and Northern Ireland Income Tax rates; Scottish taxpayers pay different rates set by the Scottish Parliament.
