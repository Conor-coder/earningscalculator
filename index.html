How Income Tax and National Insurance Work

Income Tax is charged on your earnings above a tax-free threshold called the Personal Allowance — £12,570 in 2026/27. Earnings above that are taxed at 20% (the basic rate) up to £50,270, then 40% beyond that. Both rates apply only to the slice of income within each band, not your whole salary — so earning £51,000 doesn't mean everything is taxed at 40%, only the £730 above £50,270.

National Insurance (NI) is a separate deduction that funds the NHS, state pension, and certain benefits. Class 1 employee NI — the type deducted from a salary — starts at £12,584 a year, at 8% up to £50,270 and 2% above that. Because NI and Income Tax have different starting thresholds and different rates, the effect of a pay rise on your take-home is rarely as straightforward as it looks.

Both are collected automatically by your employer through PAYE before you receive your pay — the figure in your bank account is already after both deductions.
